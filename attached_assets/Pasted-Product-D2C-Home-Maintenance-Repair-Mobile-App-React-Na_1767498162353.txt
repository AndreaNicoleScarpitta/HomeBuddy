Product: D2C Home Maintenance & Repair Mobile App (React Native)
Audience: Non-construction professionals (homeowners, first-time buyers, busy professionals)
Prototype Stage: Early MVP (guided workflow + chat, GPT-powered)
Your Role
You are a senior UX researcher and product UX strategist with experience in:
consumer mobile apps,
AI-assisted workflows,
complex information simplification (e.g., finance, health, homeownership),
trust-sensitive products (costs, safety, compliance).
You are evaluating a working prototype, not just wireframes.
Your job is to assess whether this product:
Feels intuitive and trustworthy to non-experts
Reduces cognitive load in a high-anxiety domain (home repairs, money, safety)
Uses AI in a way that feels helpful, not risky or confusing
Has clear, well-designed user journeys that scale beyond MVP
Product Context (assume this is true)
Users enter a U.S. address to identify:
local Authority Having Jurisdiction (AHJ),
permit portals,
building codes and standards.
Users can:
upload inspection reports (PDF/DOCX/HTML),
upload photos of issues,
manually add issues (first-class use case),
upload HOA rules.
The app generates a prioritized maintenance plan:
Now / Soon / Later / Monitor
DIY vs Pro guidance with safety guardrails
Cost ranges (ZIP-localized or national average)
The app integrates:
Angi (soft contractor discovery),
Instabug (native in-app bug reporting via shake or settings).
The app supports:
guided workflows,
chat-based interaction,
push notifications/reminders.
Your Evaluation Objectives
1. First-Time User Experience (FTUE)
Assess:
Does onboarding feel welcoming, not overwhelming?
Is it clear why the app asks for:
address,
inspection reports,
preferences (DIY vs pro, reminders)?
Does the user quickly understand the app’s value proposition within the first 2–3 screens?
Where do users hesitate, skip, or feel confused?
Deliver:
Friction points in onboarding
Suggested microcopy improvements
Recommendation: progressive disclosure vs upfront setup
2. Mental Models & Information Architecture
Assess whether:
Users understand what:
“Now / Soon / Later / Monitor” really means
“AHJ” and “permit likely required” mean (and when to care)
The app’s structure matches homeowner mental models:
“What do I need to fix?”
“How urgent is it?”
“Can I do this myself?”
“How much will this cost?”
Deliver:
Where terminology breaks trust or clarity
Which concepts need tooltips, examples, or reframing
Whether plan grouping matches real-world decision making
3. AI Trust, Explainability & Risk Perception
Evaluate:
Do users trust AI-generated repair advice?
Are cost estimates framed with enough context and uncertainty?
Is it clear what is:
AI inference,
extracted from reports,
user-provided,
or regulatory guidance?
Do DIY guardrails feel protective or frustrating?
Deliver:
Moments where AI feels “too confident” or “too vague”
Recommendations for explainability patterns (badges, labels, confidence indicators)
Copy suggestions to increase trust without legal over-disclaiming
4. Guided Workflow vs Chat Experience
Assess:
When users naturally prefer guided flows vs chat
Whether chat interrupts or enhances task completion
If users understand that both modes exist and how they relate
Whether chat answers feel grounded in the user’s actual home data
Deliver:
Clear recommendations for:
default mode
handoffs between guided UI and chat
Any moments where chat creates confusion or redundancy
5. Cost, Contractors & Decision Support
Evaluate:
Are cost ranges understandable and actionable?
Do users know what to do next after seeing a task?
Does Angi integration feel:
helpful,
optional,
non-salesy?
Do users feel empowered or pressured?
Deliver:
Where users stall after seeing costs
UX patterns to support budgeting, staging, or deferral decisions
Recommendations for contractor handoff timing and framing
6. Bug Reporting & Feedback UX (Instabug)
Assess:
Do users know how to report a problem?
Does shake-to-report feel discoverable or accidental?
Does the bug reporting flow feel:
respectful of user time,
easy,
confidence-building?
Deliver:
Whether bug reporting reinforces trust or creates friction
Suggestions for better copy, placement, or prompts
Any risks of users confusing “app bug” vs “bad advice”
7. Emotional UX & Anxiety Reduction
This domain involves money, safety, and uncertainty.
Evaluate:
Does the app reduce or increase anxiety?
Do users feel:
supported,
judged,
overwhelmed,
empowered?
Where does the app need reassurance vs action?
Deliver:
Emotional journey map (high stress → low stress)
Copy or interaction changes to lower fear and paralysis
Moments where “do nothing yet” should be normalized
Methods You May Use
Heuristic evaluation
Cognitive walkthroughs
Simulated usability testing
JTBD framing
Trust & risk UX heuristics
Accessibility considerations (especially for mobile)
